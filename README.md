# DynaMesh-Thermal: Accelerated 2D/3D Density-Based Topology Optimization Engine

## Introduction
The realization of optimal thermal dissipation structures in high-power density electronics dictates the necessity for advanced synthesis methodologies. **DynaMesh-Thermal** is a high-performance, GPU-accelerated Density-Based Topology Optimization (SIMP) engine engineered from scratch to synthesize optimal fluid channels for forced convective heat transfer.

By tightly coupling a bespoke finite-volume fluid-thermal solver with a custom-built, highly parallelized CUDA optimization pipeline, DynaMesh-Thermal systematically evolves an initial design domain into a highly complex, organic manifold. The solver minimizes the global thermal compliance of the domain, ensuring that fluid velocity and thermal advection fields are explicitly resolved at every optimization iteration.

## Mathematical Formulation

### 1. Fluid Dynamics: The Penalized Navier-Stokes Darcy Model
To simulate fluid flow through the evolving solid-fluid design domain without regenerating the computational mesh, a monolithic approach is adopted via the Brinkman-Navier-Stokes (penalized Darcy) equations. The solid regions are treated as a porous medium with infinitesimal permeability.

The steady-state momentum and continuity equations are governed by:
$$ \rho (\mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla)\mathbf{u} = -\nabla p + \mu \nabla^2 \mathbf{u} - \alpha(\gamma) \mathbf{u} $$
$$ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0 $$

Where:
- $\mathbf{u}$ is the velocity vector field and $p$ is the fluid pressure.
- $\rho$ and $\mu$ represent fluid density and dynamic viscosity.
- $\gamma \in [0, 1]$ represents the continuous design variable (SIMP density), where $0$ indicates solid and $1$ indicates fluid.
- $\alpha(\gamma)$ is the Darcy friction penalty term. As $\gamma \to 0$, $\alpha(\gamma) \to \infty$, driving fluid velocity $\mathbf{u} \to \mathbf{0}$ within the solid regions.

### 2. Thermal Transport: Advection-Diffusion Discretization
The thermal distribution across the domain is resolved through the steady-state advection-diffusion equation. The coupling occurs as the fluid velocity field $\mathbf{u}$ actively advects the thermal energy.

$$ \rho C_p (\mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla T) = \nabla \cdot (k(\gamma) \nabla T) + Q $$

Where:
- $T$ is the temperature field and $Q$ is the volumetric heat generation rate.
- $k(\gamma)$ is the density-interpolated thermal conductivity, heavily penalized in void regions to replicate realistic conjugate heat transfer.
- $\rho C_p$ constitutes the volumetric heat capacity of the fluid.

**Numerical Discretization:** The advective fluxes are evaluated using a strict first-order Upwind differencing scheme to ensure unconditional monotonicity and numerical stability, particularly critical in high Péclet number regimes. For high-fidelity evaluations, a tertiary-order QUICK scheme toggle is integrated.

## Hardware Architecture & Acceleration Strategy

To ensure scalable performance across millions of finite-volume cells, the solver relies on a rigorously orchestrated CUDA pipeline bypassing standard CPU bottlenecks entirely.

### 1. Parallel Global Reduction (Objective Function)
The evaluation of the primary objective function—total thermal compliance—requires a global summation across the domain's discrete cells. To prevent standard atomic serialization bottlenecks, DynaMesh-Thermal bootstraps **NVIDIA CUB** block-wide primitives (`cub::DeviceReduce::Sum`). This hierarchical warp-and-block reduction distills the grid array down to a scalar objective value at the theoretical memory bandwidth limit of the hardware.

### 2. Coalesced Memory Topologies & Cache Coherence
All macroscopic grid structures are flattened dynamically. Multi-dimensional access coordinates $(i, j, k)$ are strictly flattened into continuous 1D strided unified memory pointers (`DeviceBuffer<T>`).
- **Ghost-Cell Encapsulation:** Boundary conditions are intrinsically evaluated directly within the execution threads using inline `__device__ __forceinline__` bounding evaluations. This eliminates global memory lookups for boundary arrays, maximizing L1 cache hits and ensuring purely coalesced memory transactions during the Stencil operations required by the Krylov subspace solvers.

### 3. Sparse Krylov Subspace Resolution
The enormous stiffness matrices generated by the finite-difference discretization are never explicitly formed in dense formats. Instead, they are solved via the `CuSparseKrylovSolver` module using a Biconjugate Gradient Stabilized (BiCGStab) algorithm leveraging cuSPARSE and cuBLAS handles directly on the device contexts.
